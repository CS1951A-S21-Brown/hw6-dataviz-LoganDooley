Question 1: Graph 1 answers the question by taking the top 10 video games and clearly ranking them in the bar chart along with their respective numbers of sales for comparison. Graph 2 answers the question by having an interactive pi chart which displays the percentage of overall sales per genre depending on the region you select. The largest pie slice represents the most popular genre of the region. Graph 3 answers the prompt by displaying the top 10 publishers depending on the genre selected. To get the top publisher you only need to view the top-most bar.

Question 2: It was simple to create drawings without having to worry about rendering each individual shape in html ourselves. It was useful to group data together in a "modular" approach to simplify the logic behind what each bar/pie slice stores as data. Lastly, it is easily usable with interactive elements in html to change the graphs upon input.

Question 3: As a trade off to being easy to use, it is limited to the specific types of things precoded into the library so it is not as customizable, as in if you had a much more complicated dataset to represent, it might be difficult realize your ideas and you may need to compromise. One general con is that the documentation is not very clear which makes it somewhat difficult to use more obscure functionalities. A last disadvantage is that while d3 does a good job grouping objects, it can be tricky to keep them organized and to make sure you are calling on the correct parent object and/or selecting the right html item to call functions on.

Question 4: In response to "Accesible Data Visualization", I think the graphs are quickly understandable. Being ordered particularly in the bar charts from lowest to highest lets the reader quickly determine what the best producer for a genre is, or what the best selling video game of all time was, without having to try and guess which bar is longer than the others. One issue I ran into in regards to color pallets was picking a suitable one. I ended up deciding to have single colors for the bar chart and only different colors for the pie chart. This way there was no misleading information in the bar charts about the color indicating something it doesnt. The length of the bar tells all. The pie chart was necessary to add some color in order to differentiate the different slices, but again I made sure to keep some seperators that were white so colorblind people could discern the different segments as well.

Question 5: Before I began coding, I planned out what each graph would be, and made sure they answered the questions appropriately. One thing I struggled with non-colorwise as mentioned in the last paragraph was text and particularly labels of longer titles. This was quite difficult to choose and I think the biggest factor in there being a conflict was the dataset itself. It is quite difficult to abreviate some of these game titles into acronyms or even shorten them all properly since acronyms don't always make sense, and some games have important information at their ends such as "2" or "Wii" that differentiate them from other nearly identical ones. Had I more time I would hard code in the y axis labels for the static bar plot in graph 1, however when trying to make substrings for them it seemed to offset the bars since the y axis labels were defined within a mapping used in the y axis. The tool tip was supposed to be used to reveal the full title which it does, however these issues stopped me from shortening them.
